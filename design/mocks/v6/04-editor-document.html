On the pleasure of reading aloud

4,218 words — about 28 minutes at 1.25×, in af_heart. Last recorded 19 May.

The voice that reads aloud to itself is the most ancient of literary instruments. Long before the printed page, long before the silent eye learned its trick of swallowing meaning in a glance, there was the mouth, the breath, the unhurried turning of one syllable into the next. To listen is to be carried — to surrender the self briefly, the way a small boat surrenders to a current it cannot see beneath it. The voice that reads is not a recitation but an offering; it gives the text a body, a tempo, a place to live. What we lose in silent reading is not understanding but tempo. The page on the desk is consumed; the page read aloud is unfolded. Each word is given the time it deserves, and the listener — even when the listener is also the reader — is made to wait, and in waiting, to attend.
